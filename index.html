About Me

Hi! Welcome to my website! I'm a computational astrophysicist (in prep). My other interests include solving jigsaw puzzles, doodling, lifting weights and taking long walks. I also enjoy tinkering around in Linux and Obsidian and making my Vim look pretty.

You can find details about my work here.

work

Done with my last semester of a five-year Integrated Masters in Physics at IISER Thiruvananthapuram! I just defended my master's thesis last month and am hoping to get on stage and collect my official degree by July! My master's thesis was titled 'Impact of parameter mismodeling on gravitational-wave searches', where I tried to figure out just how badly I could model spin and eccentricity and still get away with it. (We studied the extent of spin and eccentricity mismodeling that would allow accurate parameter recovery especially in context of future gravitational-wave detectors like LISA.) My main academic interests lie in computational gravitational-wave physics, machine learning, fast parameter estimation, dark matter detection with gravitational waves, and Bayesian statistics. I am actively seeking PhD opportunities in gravitational-wave computational physics.

Right now, I have picked up my project with Prof. Takahiro Tanaka and Dr. Takahiro Yamamoto to select regions in the sky to make the detection of continuous gravitational waves easier (computationally). Prof. Tanaka and Yamamoto-san have already researched this for ground-based LIGO, and we are looking at what will happen if we consider space-based DECIGO. Will we be able to hear anything?

I also have a minor in data science, for which I worked with Dr. Apratim Ganguly and Dr. Kanchan Soni at IUCAA, Pune to explore the underlying population distribution of compact binary parameters in the deci-hertz band using predictive analysis. Our work should have significant implications for future deci-hertz detectors like DECIGO.

Before this, I got selected for the DAAD-WISE fellowship in 2022, through which I had the opportunity to work with Dr. Krishnendu Nair from Dr. Frank Ohmeâ€™s research group at MPI for Gravitational Physics, Hannover. We conducted tests of general relativity using GW data, starting with an attempt to reproduce the results of a paper bounding the mass of the graviton. Our analyses of the SNR for different masses and distances of the mergers were presented in a meeting with the group.

My interest in the field of gravitational-wave physics started with an internship in the summer of 2021 with an internship with Prof. Sanjit Mitra, where Kanchan (then his PhD student at IUCAA) helped me learn to conduct injection studies and parameter estimation using the bilby framework. She also taught me the power of Vim, and I have not looked back since (I say as I am editing this page in Vim.)

Thank you for visiting my little corner, and contact me here for any potential collaborations or discussions. I am always happy to reply to queries about internship applications and general physics-y stuff.

Outreach

Reading Between the Lines

Along with a group of motivated students, I founded an Ambedkarite reading circle at IISER Thiruvananthapuram. We get together and do book readings, and try to arrange talks and events for the campus. We try to conduct regular meetings where we discuss chapters from Dalit-Bahujan-Adivasi (DBA) literature.

Inventa Magazine

Inventa is an intercollegiate magazine between the 7 IISERs, NISER, IISc, and CEBS, that was founded in 2021. I was the second Managing Editor of the magazine from IISER Thiruvananthapuram. My team published the second edition of the magazine, the theme of which was 'Into the Future'. It explored the future of scientific innovation and of the scientific community as it strives to be more interdisciplinary. Along with my responsibilities as Managing Editor, I was also a part of the Operational Committee, where my job was to ensure seamless correspondence and collaboration between departments and that deadlines were met without fail. (We tried.)

Ether

I was an editor and a designer for IISER's literature and arts magazine, Ether. What happens when scientists are given an arts magazine? They start a conversation about how humanities is somehow lower than life sciences (it absolutely is not!) With Ether, we provided a much-needed escape from science that can sometimes be a stack of failed experiments and code that just won't run. Although I did write for the magazine, most of my work was in proofreading the editions and typesetting the magazine copies in Adobe InDesign.

Exhibit A

Exhibit A was founded in 2019, right after I joined college, and I was lucky enough to be part of this initiative since its inception. Since I have watched it grow throughout the years (and through COVID), this newsletter holds a special place in my heart. I started as a writer for this newsletter, and was later editor and designer for it. This experience made me appreciate my interest in science communication and taught me how Dr. and Prof. is punctuated.

Club of Mathematics, IISER Thiruvananthapuram

In my first year of college, I was a coordinator for the official mathematics club of IISER. I helped compile the by-laws and the foundational groundwork for the club. Although all members of the club was extremely welcoming and receptive of my ideas, being one of the only two girls in the coordinators group made me realise how skewed (and screwed) the gender parity was in math. (I would go on to realise that this was the case for most of academia.)
